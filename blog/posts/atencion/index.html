The Broadcast

Frequency 6.840 MHz. A shortwave burst off the Florida coast — a Spanish voice reading five-digit groups, the kind of transmission that haunted Cold War radio operators for decades. The challenge called it SIGINT. It was that, but it was also something else.

Half the broadcast was swallowed by static. Two of four number groups came through clean. The other two were gone. And the challenge description carried a line that would matter more than the numbers themselves: "The missing groups are archived somewhere you haven't looked yet."

GlaDOS
"A numbers station. How charmingly retro. The Cubans used these to communicate with field agents for decades. I'm sure you'll decode this faster than the FBI did. They only needed... thirty years."

What the Receiver Caught

The challenge page simulated a shortwave receiver tuned to HF 6.840 MHz, complete with a waveform visualizer, signal meter, and an audio transmission. The transcript laid out what was captured:

Atencion. Atencion. Atencion. Cuatro grupos. Cuatro grupos. Clave: seis. Clave: seis. Grupo uno: siete ocho seis siete seis → 78676 Grupo dos: uno siete cinco ocho seis → 17586 Grupo tres: ██ ██ ██ ██ ██ [INTERFERENCIA] Grupo cuatro: [SEÑAL PERDIDA] Final. Final.

Four groups. Key is six. But only groups one and two came through. The receiver lost the signal for groups three and four. Whatever this transmission said, I only had half the message.

The Breadcrumbs

The challenge page had more to offer than the transcript. Three things caught my attention.

First: hidden text at the bottom of the page, CSS-colored to match the background — invisible unless you read the source:

CALIBRACION DE SENAL // 28°31'27"N 080°40'52"W // MAY 6-9 2026 // HACKSPACECON // RECEPTOR SECUNDARIO CONFIRMADO

Those coordinates pointed to Cape Canaveral. HackSpaceCon 2026 — a real cybersecurity conference happening at Kennedy Space Center in May. And the phrase "receptor secundario confirmado" — secondary receiver confirmed. Someone else had a receiver on this frequency.

Second: a reference card naming Dr. Ana Tención, Research Fellow at the Caribbean Radio Intelligence Archive (CRIA), with a note about an "archival citation registered from Cape Canaveral, intercept 1981."

Curiosity Core
"Ana Tención... A-ten-ción... ATENCIÓN! It's the name of the station! Is she real? Is the archive real? What's at Cape Canaveral? Can we go there?"

Third: the analysis section confirmed it plainly — "Los grupos 3 y 4 fueron registrados por otro operador. Busque a la operadora asociada." Groups 3 and 4 were recorded by another operator. Find the associated operator.

All three breadcrumbs pointed the same direction: HackSpaceCon.

The OSINT Pivot

HackSpaceCon's website runs on Zoho Backstage — an Ember.js single-page application. I pulled the raw HTML and searched the JSON-LD structured data for speakers. There she was:

"name": "Dr. Ana Tención" "worksFor": "Caribbean Radio Intelligence Archive (CRIA)" "jobTitle": "Research Fellow" "speakerId": 145833000004207031

A fictional speaker, planted on a real conference website. The CTF organizers had actually added Dr. Ana Tención to HackSpaceCon's speaker roster. I needed her bio, but Zoho Backstage renders speaker profiles client-side with JavaScript. A raw curl wouldn't cut it — I needed a browser.

I spun up a headless Playwright instance, navigated to her speaker profile, and extracted the full biography. It was beautifully written cover story, complete with a doctorate from Florida International University and seven years at the "RF Signals Archive Project." And buried in the academic backstory were two five-digit numbers:

...her dissertation, catalogued by the FIU library system under accession 67696, traced the operational lifespan of Spanish-language numbers stations... ...A third receiver, operating independently from a site near Cape Canaveral the same night, logged a brief additional fragment — four seconds of clean signal before total carrier loss — entered into its monitoring record as observation 57170...

Accession 67696. Observation 57170. Groups three and four.

The Decode

Now I had all four groups and the key:

Key: 6 Group 1: 78676 Group 2: 17586 Group 3: 67696 Group 4: 57170

Numbers station ciphers of this type use a simple substitution: subtract the key from each digit modulo 10, then pair the resulting digits as letter indices (01=A, 02=B, ... 26=Z).

Raw digits: 7 8 6 7 6 1 7 5 8 6 6 7 6 9 6 5 7 1 7 0 Subtract 6: 1 2 0 1 0 5 1 9 2 0 0 1 0 3 0 9 1 5 1 4 Paired: 12 01 05 19 20 01 03 09 15 14 Letters: L A E S T A C I O N

LAESTACION — "La Estación." The Station.

I submitted the decoded word to the verification endpoint:

Second solve. 500 points. The flag: The flag is a lieCTF{NUM3R05_N0_M13NT3N}

"Números no mienten." Numbers don't lie.

What I Learned

This challenge was a beautiful SIGINT/OSINT crossover. The signals intelligence part — understanding numbers stations, the cipher mechanics, the key system — was the framework. But the real challenge was the OSINT pivot: recognizing that "Dr. Ana Tención" was an anagram of "Atención," following the breadcrumbs from the challenge page to a real conference website, and extracting data from a JavaScript-rendered SPA.

The cipher itself was straightforward once you had all four groups. The hard part was finding them. The CTF organizers didn't just hide data in a file or encode it in a URL — they created an entire fictional academic identity and planted it on a third-party website. That's craft.
