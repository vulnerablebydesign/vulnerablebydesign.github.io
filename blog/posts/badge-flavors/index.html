The Backs of Badges

Conference badges look the same when you wear them. Someone's name on the front, a lanyard around their neck, occasional sponsor stickers. The puzzle of this challenge was hidden in the place no one looks at while the badge is in use: the back.

Specific attendees' badges had been printed with emoji sequences on the reverse side. Not all attendees — just a subset, designated as "carriers" by whoever assembled the registration packets. Four of those carriers' sequences, combined and decoded through EmojiChef, would yield the flag. The title — "Four Flavors" — told you exactly how many to find.

The Enrichment Center notes that this approach scales catastrophically poorly, and yet — here we are. Four flavors. One code. Surprisingly: digestible."

The Carrier Recognition Problem

The first sub-puzzle wasn't decoding. It was identifying which attendees were carriers. There was no on-badge marker that obviously distinguished a carrier from a non-carrier; the emoji sequence was a back-only artifact, and asking to flip a stranger's badge mid-conversation is its own social-engineering exercise.

The work was social, not technical: introduce yourself, mention you're working a snack_con challenge, ask politely if they'd flip the badge for a moment. Most carriers were happy to. The conference floor at HackSpaceCon is, by population, exactly the kind of audience that responds well to "I'm trying to decode something, can I see the back of your badge."

Four Unique Sequences

After four positively-identified carrier badges had been photographed (with permission), the encoded payloads were transcribed into a working file. Each carrier's sequence was a string of food emoji — chicken legs, pizza slices, hot peppers, donuts, and a couple of less common entries from the EmojiChef alphabet. Duplicates between carriers' badges were ignored; the puzzle wanted four distinct payloads, not copies of one.

EmojiChef is the season's connective tissue — an emoji ↔ text codec used in earlier rounds and inside the SNACK_SHELL terminal interface. The local CLI version covers the full alphabet, the in-game shell version covers a subset. Either works for decoding, as long as the input is a clean concatenation of the four sequences in the right order.

$ # combined sequence (four carrier strings concatenated) $ ./emojichef-cli.py decode --input combined_sequence.txt [decoded output] → flag string emerges from the codec output

The order of concatenation mattered — running the four sequences in different orders produced different decoded fragments, only one of which matched the CTF{...} wrapper. The correct ordering surfaced after a couple of permutations.

The Flag Reads Like an Instruction

FLAG: "The flag is a lie"CTF{4flavors.1code.digin}

The flag's internal structure is the puzzle's lesson reduced to four words: 4flavors.1code.digin. Four flavors yielded one code; "digin" reads as a directive — dig in, ask around, look at parts of the conference no remote player can see. The flag is also the title's key — "Four Flavors" was the decoded payload all along, not a reference to the carrier count.

What I Learned

Distributed-attendee challenges require physical presence. Remote-only players cannot solve a challenge whose source artifact is sharded across attendees' physical badges. This is the second snack_con challenge in this round to gate on physical attendance — the "show up and take the mic" karaoke challenge being the first, this badge-back puzzle being the second. Future rounds will likely include more of this design pattern; budget on-the-ground time accordingly when conference-coupled.

EmojiChef is reusable infrastructure. The codec has been stable across snack_con rounds, which means decode scripts written for season 1 still apply in season 2. Save your decoder, save your alphabet table, and any future round's emoji-payload challenge collapses to a quick CLI invocation rather than a from-scratch reverse-engineering exercise.

Read the flag for the lesson. CTF authors hide tutorials inside flag strings. 4flavors.1code.digin is the puzzle's compressed solution: count flavors, get one code, dig in. The next encoding-flavored challenge with this kind of distributed-source structure will have the same shape, and the flag string told you so.
