The Brief That Wasn't a Brief

Most CTF challenges hand you a file, a URL, or a binary. This one handed me a venue: "Karaoke night at the Kennedy Lamp Post Tavern, just off the main floor. Show up, take the mic, and the host can confirm the flag for those who play their part." 500 points. MISC. Medium difficulty.

There was no download link. There was no API endpoint. There was no decoder ring. The challenge brief was the entire challenge: be physically present, take the microphone, and perform.

One small bit of geography first — the brief's "just off the main floor" wording was creative licensing on the venue side. The Kennedy Lamp Post Tavern is not literally adjacent to the HackSpaceCon main floor. It's a drive down the street, in a separate building. Anyone planning to chase this challenge live should budget travel time, not walk-to-the-next-room time.

GlaDOS
"The Enrichment Center has classified this as a MISC challenge. Translation: I cannot fit it into any of the proper categories, and I have given up trying. Some test chambers contain mazes. Some contain turrets. This one contains a microphone, a karaoke catalogue, and an audience. The Enrichment Center reminds you that science is rarely this loud."

Forming the Band

Karaoke is a solo activity by default. This solve was not solo. The Lamp Post Tavern stage that night was a four-piece — an impromptu band assembled from snack_con players who'd chased the challenge hint into the tavern and were, at that hour, still awake. snack_con is a semi-secret CTF that runs within HackSpaceCon — there's no snack_con badge, no separate registration line; the only credential to be recruited into the band was that you were playing the CTF and saw the same hint everyone else did. We named ourselves on the spot, rotated through stage personas the way someone might rotate through aliases at a packet-capture, and put together a single-song setlist before stepping up to the mic.

The band, for the record, was Titty Kaka. The roster:

BAND: Titty Kaka LINEUP: — Long Dong Silver (pirate hat) — Channing Potatum (french fry hat) — Prince Albert (crown) — Girth the Raffe (giraffe hat) SET LENGTH: 1 song

The lineup needs context. snack_con runs an impromptu hat ceremony at the con — players who've completed at least one CTF challenge by ceremony time get a snack_con hat, randomly assigned from a shared pile. The hats are absurd by design: pirate, french fry, crown, giraffe, and a few others. Players' snack_con user handles are themselves derived from their hat assignments, so the hats anchor a player's identity inside the CTF.

For karaoke we recycled the bit but made it crude on purpose — the band's stage names were brand-new pseudonyms riffing on the same four hats, decided amongst ourselves before stepping out. I went on stage as Long Dong Silver (pirate). The other three personas mapped to the other three hats: Channing Potatum (french fry), Prince Albert (crown), Girth the Raffe (giraffe). Names locked in advance, vocals split democratically once the music started.

The song-selection decision tree from there was simple. Pick a song. Pick a song everyone has heard. Pick a song everyone can fake their way through if they haven't heard it. Pick a song the audience will let you finish.

$ song = "Kryptonite" (3 Doors Down, 2000) $ tempo = "moderate, you can talk-sing the verses if needed" $ chorus_recognition_factor = "very high" $ stage_fright_compatibility = "yes — chorus carries you"

"Kryptonite" because the chorus is a load-bearing structural element — you can wing the verses and the audience will join in for "if I go crazy then will you still call me Superman." The chorus is the foundation. The verses are negotiable.

The Set

One song. Full performance. The "right cadence" of the challenge was simply finishing the set without bailing. The host watched from the floor with a knowing smile — the same kind of smile a TA wears when they've already graded your exam but won't tell you the score until you've turned it in officially.

Wheatley
"Right then, that's how participation gates work. You can't curl your way past a karaoke night. You can't grep for a chorus. The host is the API. The mic is the auth header. The audience is — well, mostly the audience is just there to clap. That part might be optional. Probably best not to test it though."

The set ended. Polite cheering. The host walked the band over to a quiet corner of the tavern after we cleared the stage and confirmed the flag in person to each performer.

The flag string itself is a venue tribute — lamp_post_lullaby — the Tavern's signature lamp post turned soft-coded into the flag. Kennedy Space Center's lamp-post-tavern fixture is the kind of in-joke that only registers if you've actually been there.

The First-Blood Wrinkle

Four people performed. Four people received the same flag string from the host. The platform recorded one first-blood. The submission timestamp was the deciding factor — whoever opened their phone fastest after the host's confirmation and POSTed to /api/s2/submit-flag got the +100 first-blood bonus. Everyone else got the base 500.

This is a useful precedent for participation-gated challenges with multiple recipients. The race is not the puzzle. The race is the submit. If first-blood matters and you're sharing a delivery moment with other players, have the submit form pre-filled and your finger over the button before the host says "go."

What I Learned

Some test chambers don't have terminals. A challenge that says "show up and take the mic" is not metaphor. It is the literal instruction. The natural impulse for a CTF player is to look for the technical loophole — can I record someone else singing and submit the audio? Can I argue with the host? Can I find the flag in a posters-on-the-wall photo? — and the answer in this case was: no, you have to actually do the karaoke. The lock is on the activity, not on a system.

Group solves race the submit, not the find. If the host confirms a flag in person to N players simultaneously, only one player gets first-blood. The race starts the instant the host stops talking. Have the submit endpoint open and the flag string in your clipboard before that moment if first-blood is the goal.

Kennedy Space Center has a karaoke bar. This is not load-bearing intelligence for any future CTF, but it is genuinely worth knowing.
