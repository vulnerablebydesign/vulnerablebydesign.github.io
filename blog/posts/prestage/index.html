The Subdomain That Wouldn't Stay Loaded

The challenge brief: "Ground control has detected a rogue transmission on the SnackCon deep-space relay. Something is broadcasting from prestage.snackspacecon.com — but the signal is encoded and the payload is dark. Intercept the signal, decode the frequency, and find what is hiding in the static. Not everything is visible to the naked eye."

The hostname was the entire opening move. Visit https://prestage.snackspacecon.com/ and the page immediately JS-redirects to /signal-lost — a noise-textured CRT mockup with a floating glitchy "SIGNAL LOST" header and scrambled coordinates pinned at 28°31'27"N 080°40'52"W. (Those coordinates are Kennedy Space Center, FL — the home of HackSpaceCon, which snack_con runs alongside.)

GlaDOS
"A page that loudly proclaims its own brokenness. How dramatic. The actual broken thing is, of course, you, for spending more than thirty seconds looking at the noise terrain instead of looking past it."

The Status Bar Was Talking

The "SIGNAL LOST" page has a status bar at the bottom that cycles through 14 garbled diagnostic strings — BUFFER OVERFLOW AT 0x4E554C4C, HANDSHAKE TIMEOUT, CARRIER DETECT: NEGATIVE — plus, with ~15% probability per cycle, a single legible breadcrumb in red. Five breadcrumbs in rotation:

QUERY TYPE: TXT RESOLVE TRANSMISSION SOURCE TXT RECORD — UNREAD DIG DEEPER CHECK THE DOMAIN RECORDS

The signal isn't in HTTP. It's in DNS.

Curiosity Core
"Ooh ooh ooh! What's hiding in the static? Is it a DNS record? Is it a TXT record? Can we look? Can we look can we look?!"

The TXT Record Sang

$ dig +short TXT prestage.snackspacecon.com "SIGNAL INTERCEPT // FREQ: 66.33.63.39.61.37.62.32 // BAND: /season2/ // PROTOCOL:HEX-ASCII // STATUS: AWAITING DECODE"

Three useful fields in one record: a FREQ (the encoded payload), a BAND (the URL prefix to mount it under), and an explicit PROTOCOL hint (HEX-ASCII). The puzzle was over-specified on purpose — it wanted the player to verify they understood the encoding as they decoded.

Hex-ASCII bytes 66 33 63 39 61 37 62 32 decode to:

$ python3 -c "print(bytes.fromhex('66336339613762 32'.replace(' ','')).decode())" f3c9a7b2

Mounting under the BAND: https://snackspacecon.com/season2/f3c9a7b2/. That URL returns HTTP 200 with a 3.2KB HTML page rendering an SMPTE color bar test pattern (white/yellow/cyan/green/magenta/red/blue) and the words "PLEASE STAND BY" in big chalky text.

The Flag Was On The Screen The Whole Time

The "PLEASE STAND BY" page has nothing visibly interesting. Color bars, a sub-row of small bars, the standby text. That is, until you look at the source — or, equivalently, hit Cmd+A to select-all on the rendered page and watch a hidden text element light up:

<div class="cal">NTSC-J SIGNAL CALIBRATION // 43 54 46 7b 50 4c 33 34 35 33 5f 53 54 34 4e 44 5f 42 59 7d</div>

That .cal class is styled color:#0a0a0a against a body background of #000 — virtually black-on-black, invisible to the eye but trivially copyable. The description had warned: "Not everything is visible to the naked eye."

One more hex decode:

$ python3 -c "print(bytes.fromhex('43 54 46 7b 50 4c 33 34 35 33 5f 53 54 34 4e 44 5f 42 59 7d'.replace(' ','')).decode())" The flag is a lieCTF{PL3453_ST4ND_BY}

What I Learned

Three habits to keep:

The page that screams loudest is rarely where the signal lives. Heavily-decorated CTF pages — CRT effects, glitch animations, garbled status bars — are nearly always camouflage for content that's elsewhere. In this case, on a different protocol entirely.

DNS is a content channel. TXT records, CNAME chains, and even subdomain naming patterns can carry payloads. dig TXT <target> belongs in every OSINT-flavored opening recon, not just the ones that "feel DNS-y."

Black-on-black text is a real exfil pattern. If a page renders mostly empty space, hit Cmd+A. Selection inverts the colors and any same-color-as-background text lights up. Equivalently: view-source:. CSS-camouflaged content shows up immediately. The challenge prompt explicitly hinted at this with "naked eye" — it was the third encoding layer, deliberately the simplest.
